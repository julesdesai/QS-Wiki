# Can true belief from a reliable process still lack knowledge due to ignorance of key details?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 5aefaebd-e2e8-4ba4-a697-1ab238984bc3
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Reliability Theory of Knowledge](reliability-theory-of-knowledge-synthesis-74a39329-93a9-479f-b8d3-ff182da0738f.md)

## Content

**Consider a scenario where a person is in a region known for magnetic anomalies that can alter compass readings without them knowing. If they use a compass to determine the north and it happens to be correct, intuitively, they lack certainty about their direction.**, **The cognitive process involved—using a compass—typically yields true beliefs under normal conditions. Thus, this belief could be viewed as knowledge.**, **The reliability of the process is compromised by unseen variables, making the true belief accidental, which questions its status as knowledge.**, **Knowledge requires not only a reliable process but also awareness of conditions rendering the process unreliable, complicating the reliability theory of knowledge.**
