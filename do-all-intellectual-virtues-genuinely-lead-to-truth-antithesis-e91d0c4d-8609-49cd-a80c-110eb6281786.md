# Do all intellectual virtues genuinely lead to truth?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** e91d0c4d-8609-49cd-a80c-110eb6281786
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Virtue Epistemology View](virtue-epistemology-view-synthesis-28ac8895-184f-4b0d-8051-e8e35e6bae56.md)

## Content

**Suppose we have a case in which someone has a trait considered an intellectual virtue, like being highly imaginative, which sometimes leads away from truth rather than towards it. Intuitively, we either have to say that this trait is not an intellectual virtue or that not all intellectual virtues reliably lead to true beliefs. If we say it is not an intellectual virtue, then we ignore the commonly held positive value of traits like imagination. If we say not all intellectual virtues lead to true beliefs, then this undermines the claim that intellectual virtues are defined by reliable truth.
**, **There is a plausible principle that a trait leading to reliably false beliefs cannot constitute an intellectual virtue. This principle together with the possibility that some traditionally valued traits do not invariably lead to truth entails a challenge to defining intellectual virtues.**
