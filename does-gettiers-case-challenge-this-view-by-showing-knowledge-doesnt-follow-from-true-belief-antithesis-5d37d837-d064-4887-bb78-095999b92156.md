# Does Gettier's case challenge this view by showing knowledge doesn't follow from true belief?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 5d37d837-d064-4887-bb78-095999b92156
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Externalism with Causal Theory](externalism-with-causal-theory-synthesis-67f241d3-d718-4f9e-97b2-c0aff473bbc3.md)

## Content

**Consider a scenario where someone forms a belief based on misleading evidence, such as seeing a clock that stopped an hour ago but accidentally indicates the correct time. The person believes it is the correct time and this belief matches reality because the stopped clock coincidentally indicates the right time. Intuitively, this belief does not qualify as knowledge.**, **A person can have a true belief without accessing the justification for their belief, yet still lack knowledge if the belief formation process is flawed, as demonstrated by the clock example.**
