# Does the role of intellectual virtue adequately cover all forms of knowledge?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 71240563-1b3f-478b-9ab2-e99156b51907
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Virtue Epistemology](virtue-epistemology-synthesis-c68e0504-3343-450c-a9ae-cebbb34ec2ad.md)

## Content

**There is a plausible principle that suggests some forms of practical knowledge, such as knowing how to ride a bicycle, differ fundamentally from purely propositional knowledge like knowing a fact. This principle together with the focus on intellectual virtue, which is typically propositional, entails that certain non-propositional forms of knowledge might not be adequately accounted for by intellectual virtue**, **Suppose we have a case in which someone knows how to ride a bicycle through practice rather than intellectual reflection. Intuitively, we either have to say that this is knowledge based on intellectual virtue, or it’s a different kind of knowledge. If we say it depends on intellectual virtue, then it overly intellectualizes practical knowledge. If we say it differs, then intellectual virtue might not encompass all knowledge types**
