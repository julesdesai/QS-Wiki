# Can knowledge arise from cognitive abilities that are not virtues?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 72b909e2-ef28-403f-84e4-ad1c201df89e
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Virtue Epistemology](virtue-epistemology-synthesis-40b3d691-a5bb-479c-ada3-1931521a86e5.md)

## Content

**Imagine a situation where a person forms a true belief through quick intuition rather than deliberative intellectual virtue. Intuitively, one might judge that the person knows the truth even though the belief did not arise from a recognized intellectual virtue.**, **A belief can be accurate due to cognitive traits that do not fit the standard conception of virtue, like quick intuitive leaps or lucky guesses.**, **In many instances, cognitive abilities that are not seen as intellectual virtues can lead to true beliefs, calling into question whether knowledge always arises from intellectual virtue.**
