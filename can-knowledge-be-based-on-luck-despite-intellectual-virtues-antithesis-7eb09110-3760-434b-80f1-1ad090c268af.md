# Can knowledge be based on luck despite intellectual virtues?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 7eb09110-3760-434b-80f1-1ad090c268af
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Virtue Epistemology](virtue-epistemology-synthesis-c68e0504-3343-450c-a9ae-cebbb34ec2ad.md)

## Content

**Consider a scenario where a person forms a true belief about the location of their lost keys because someone else, who is unreliable, happened to guess the correct location. In this scenario, intuitively we would judge that the person holding the true belief does not know the keys' location, even though it might seem an intellectual virtue if they used cognitive faculties appropriately**, **There is a plausible principle that knowledge should not be achieved through mere luck. This principle together with an understanding that a belief can be both true and gained by an exercise of intellectual virtue entails that the definition of knowledge might include cases of lucky guesses, which contradicts the aim of excluding luck from knowledge**
