# How can disjunctivism account for hallucinations?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 46f2e1a5-79e9-491d-bbd7-fa0f32881faa
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Disjunctivism in Epistemology](disjunctivism-in-epistemology-synthesis-4a1da673-a89c-4ba0-b895-eeed98d21cef.md)

## Content

**Consider a scenario in which a person has a hallucination, believing they see a red apple in front of them when in fact there is no apple. In such a scenario, intuitively we judge that the person still believes they are justified in believing in the apple's existence.**, **If perceptual experiences justify beliefs only if veridically connected, then this hallucination provides no justification.**, **It seems implausible that a common belief like the experience of an apple does not provide any justification, even when it is a hallucination.**
