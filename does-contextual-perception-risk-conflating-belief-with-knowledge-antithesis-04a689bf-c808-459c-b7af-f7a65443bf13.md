# Does contextual perception risk conflating belief with knowledge?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 04a689bf-c808-459c-b7af-f7a65443bf13
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Contextualism](contextualism-synthesis-b8a53bd2-d0b8-44ba-a658-8cb1f890a45a.md)

## Content

**Suppose we have a case where a person believes a proposition based on their specific context, while another person in a different context does not share this belief. Intuitively, we either have to say that both have knowledge, or that the belief in one context is mistaken. If we say both have knowledge, it challenges the universality of knowledge. If we say one belief is mistaken, it challenges the validity of the contextualist view.**, **A plausible principle is that belief and knowledge are distinct. This principle, together with the idea that context heavily influences perception, might lead to equating belief with knowledge unjustifiably.**
