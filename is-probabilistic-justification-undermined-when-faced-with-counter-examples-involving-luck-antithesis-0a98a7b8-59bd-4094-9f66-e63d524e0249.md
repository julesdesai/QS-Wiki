# Is probabilistic justification undermined when faced with counter-examples involving luck?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 0a98a7b8-59bd-4094-9f66-e63d524e0249
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Probabilistic Foundationalism](probabilistic-foundationalism-synthesis-16fe8fa2-69bc-4186-a85e-e3861ba3ba16.md)

## Content

**Consider someone who justifiably believes, based on sound statistical data, that it will rain tomorrow, yet the actual cause of rain is anomalous and unforeseeable shifts. Intuitively, this eventual true belief still doesn’t constitute knowledge.**, **The role of luck in the realization of a true belief, even when probabilistically justified, suggests an intuition that something crucial is absent for knowledge.**, **Knowledge seems to require overcoming serendipity or random chance, challenging the idea that probability alone suffices.**
