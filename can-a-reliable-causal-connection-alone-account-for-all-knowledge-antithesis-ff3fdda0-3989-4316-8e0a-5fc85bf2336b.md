# Can a reliable causal connection alone account for all knowledge?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** ff3fdda0-3989-4316-8e0a-5fc85bf2336b
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Causal Theory of Knowledge](causal-theory-of-knowledge-synthesis-2b44e1eb-9f6a-4c4d-97b0-9b782ad8c2af.md)

## Content

**There is a plausible principle that if a belief is reliably caused by the truth, it should count as knowledge. This principle together with the possibility of environmental coincidences, where a belief is true and reliably caused but not based on the actual state of affairs, entails that not all such beliefs should count as knowledge.**, **Consider a scenario in which a person sees a fake barn façade in an area filled with fake barns and by sheer luck looks at the one real barn. Intuitively, we would judge that although this person has a true belief reliably caused by seeing the barn, it does not constitute knowledge.**
