# Can a belief targeted by external interference still be considered safe and thus knowledge?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** e5a5c079-7a7d-46cd-a798-dc07c19b7995
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Knowledge as Safety](knowledge-as-safety-synthesis-32d99a7a-9598-4d4c-84b2-a8ab3d13c5e9.md)

## Content

**Suppose we have a case in which a person, due to a temporary satellite malfunction, believes a false weather report that predicts rain, yet it rains due to an unrelated front. Intuitively, we either have to say that the person knows it will rain because they believe truly, or they do not know due to external interference misleading them. If we say they know, then knowledge does not appropriately exclude externally targeted interference. If we say they do not know, then knowledge requires stability from external interference.**, **There is a plausible principle that knowledge excludes circumstances in which interference could easily have made the belief false. This principle together with the idea of epistemic safety entails that knowledge requires more than true belief.**
