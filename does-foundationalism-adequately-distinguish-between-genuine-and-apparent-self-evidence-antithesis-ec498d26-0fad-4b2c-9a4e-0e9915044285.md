# Does foundationalism adequately distinguish between genuine and apparent self-evidence?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** ec498d26-0fad-4b2c-9a4e-0e9915044285
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Foundationalism: Basic Beliefs](foundationalism-basic-beliefs-synthesis-fa10f2ed-646f-4b84-85d3-ce2c6a58459d.md)

## Content

**Genuine self-evidence involves beliefs that are truly self-evident, requiring no external justification.**, **Apparent self-evidence might seem obvious but can be mistaken or influenced by cognitive biases.**, **A belief like 'the shortest path between two points is a straight line' may seem universally self-evident but can be questioned in non-Euclidean geometry.**, **If foundationalism fails to differentiate genuine from apparent self-evidence, it risks grounding justification in potentially flawed beliefs.**
