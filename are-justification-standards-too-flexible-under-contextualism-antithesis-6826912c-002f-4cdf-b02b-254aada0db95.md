# Are justification standards too flexible under contextualism?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 6826912c-002f-4cdf-b02b-254aada0db95
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Contextualist Epistemology](contextualist-epistemology-synthesis-771a8ab1-0860-48e1-822c-9b3b2cda1c63.md)

## Content

**It is implausible that knowledge can justifiably exist in both ordinary and skeptical contexts without a substantive difference in justification.**, **Suppose we have a case in which a person claims to know a widely accepted scientific theory in everyday conversation, but the same claim is challenged in a philosophical discussion posing skeptical doubts. Intuitively, it seems unjustified to say their knowledge is equally valid in both contexts without more robust justification in the latter.**, **Consider a scenario in which the same piece of evidence is sufficient to know a fact in one context but not another. Intuitively, this seems problematic, as it undermines the consistency of knowledge claims.**
