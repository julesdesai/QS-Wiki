# Can context adequately explain all cases of justified belief?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** d5c2795a-d479-46b6-8747-1e5d977ce854
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Contextualism Summary](contextualism-summary-synthesis-4f1ad7a2-76eb-4161-89f7-f4b83097a73b.md)

## Content

**Consider a scenario in which someone accurately perceives a dog in the park and believes there is a dog in the park, but their belief is based on a mistaken context, such as thinking they are in a different park. It is implausible that this person truly has justification in that incorrect context.**, **There is a plausible principle that justification should be independent of contextual mistakes. This principle suggests that even if the context were different, the justification should remain consistent and stable.**, **In the mistaken park scenario, intuitively, we would judge that the person does not have true justified belief, challenging whether context-dependence is sufficient as a condition for knowledge.**
