# Should the accessibility of evidence solely to external observers imply genuine knowledge for the believer?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 78e3f46d-1d75-455d-89b8-b86fbc18d6d7
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Evidential Externalism](evidential-externalism-synthesis-1f3be30d-cfb3-46d2-9d1a-294e056d7ea0.md)

## Content

**A case in which a person's belief is considered knowledge solely because the external evidence is convincing to external observers, without the person's awareness, raises intuitive concern. In such a case, we intuitively judge that the person's belief does not amount to knowledge because the evidence is not integrated into their cognitive perspective.**, **There is a plausible principle that for something to count as knowledge for an individual, the evidence must affect the individual's justification process, suggesting that external evidence alone does not suffice.**
