# Does relying solely on testimony about experiences undermine first-hand knowledge?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 1dd97184-a944-4d6e-8456-a542ba37e192
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Moderate Phenomenalism](moderate-phenomenalism-synthesis-66f89d49-d085-4cff-b6cd-a9e130c91f7b.md)

## Content

**There is a plausible principle that first-hand knowledge holds more epistemic value than derived knowledge. This principle together with the notion of relying on testimony as the main source entails that much of our supposed knowledge lacks robust foundations.**, **Suppose we have a case in which a person primarily knows about the world through stories told by others rather than through their own experience. Intuitively, we either have to say that their knowledge is suspect and possibly incomplete, or that testimony is sufficient to confer full knowledge. If we say testimony is sufficient, then first-hand experience loses its significance. If we say knowledge is suspect, this raises questions about the reliability of indirect knowledge.**
