# Does the Gettier problem challenge the sufficiency of true belief for knowledge?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** f51e4f57-8ca8-4719-b23e-2fac4ff4575f
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Evidentialist Foundationalism](evidentialist-foundationalism-synthesis-26c84477-fc91-4190-8a0e-24d90c56393c.md)

## Content

**Consider a scenario in which a person has a justified belief that a colleague will get the job based on strong evidence, and it turns out to be true, but only because the person was misled by a truth about a different colleague. In such a scenario, intuitively we would judge that the person did not truly "know" who got the job, indicating that justified true belief is not sufficient for knowledge.**, **A justified true belief can result from circumstances involving luck or coincidence, which do not seem to fit our ordinary understanding of "knowledge". Intuitively, knowledge should not be the result of luck.**, **Traditional accounts of knowledge require the elimination of all cases where justification coincides with truth due to luck rather than a causal or evidential connection. Evidentialist foundationalism must account for this discrepancy.**
