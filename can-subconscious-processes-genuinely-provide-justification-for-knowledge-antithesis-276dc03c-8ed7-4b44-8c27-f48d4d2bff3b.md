# Can subconscious processes genuinely provide justification for knowledge?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 276dc03c-8ed7-4b44-8c27-f48d4d2bff3b
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Epistemic Externalism](epistemic-externalism-synthesis-3f203114-9a4a-4d29-9451-dfb5538c3c76.md)

## Content

**Consider a scenario in which a person is scared of dogs but is not consciously aware of this fear. In such a scenario, intuitively we would judge that the person does not have justification for their fear.**, **Suppose we have a case in which someone solves a math problem due to subconscious processing. Intuitively, we either have to say that they knew the answer or that they arrived at it accidentally. If we say they knew it, then we assume subconscious processes can justify knowledge. If we say it was accidental, then subconscious processing does not constitute knowledge.**
