# Is knowledge truly invariant across contexts when justification standards fluctuate?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 3b504fc3-cf43-4e15-8fa1-a199a01a9e92
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Contextualism in Knowledge Justification](contextualism-in-knowledge-justification-synthesis-d6d15e12-9924-4e45-93dc-655c9b790af5.md)

## Content

**Consider a scenario where a person is in an informal discussion at a coffee shop and claims to know that their friend is at the library because they saw them there yesterday. Intuitively, we might judge that this person knows their friend is at the library within this context.**, **Imagine the same person later in a high-stakes legal setting where the location of their friend is crucial to an alibi. Here, intuitively, we might not judge that the same evidence suffices to claim knowledge.**, **The standards for justification seem to affect whether we attribute knowledge to a person, even with the same underlying belief and truth conditions.**, **It is implausible to consistently maintain that knowledge is simply justified true belief when what counts as justification is so heavily context-dependent.**
