# Does the truth of a proposition directly imply knowledge?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** bb503e93-55d3-4c53-8e5a-24fa7c382145
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Virtue Epistemology Summary](virtue-epistemology-summary-synthesis-eb390c86-5ce7-412f-bf22-00e631bfa2b2.md)

## Content

**The truth of a proposition is a necessary condition for knowledge, but not sufficient on its own. It is implausible to suggest that a proposition's truth entails knowledge without further conditions being met.**, **Consider the lottery paradox where someone bets on a specific ticket not winning. The proposition that the ticket will not win is true because it's statistically probable, but it is not knowledge since it's based on probability, not certainty.**, **In a scenario where a person claims to know a fair coin will land on heads due to chance, and it happens to be true, intuitive judgment suggests that they do not possess knowledge despite the proposition being true.**
