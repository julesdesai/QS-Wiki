# Does reliability adequately account for the social and contextual aspects of knowledge?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 7f0f4338-613f-4d96-8dd0-71f9256d9654
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Reliability Theory of Knowledge](reliability-theory-of-knowledge-synthesis-74a39329-93a9-479f-b8d3-ff182da0738f.md)

## Content

**Suppose a person gains a true belief about a scientific fact through a reliable source—a reputable news outlet. Intuitively, they may or may not fully understand the context or epistemic community’s consensus on the issue.**, **Knowledge involves not only the reliability of the initial belief-forming process but also social context and agreement among experts, which affects its status as knowledge.**, **Different communities might assess the reliability of sources differently, impacting whether the belief is classified as knowledge.**, **This challenge implies that reliability might not fully capture the social dimensions necessary for understanding what counts as knowledge.**
