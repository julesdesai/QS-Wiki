# Can contextualism adequately account for the possibility of error even in everyday contexts?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** be1e9645-0476-4466-88d8-5fd85cec4413
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Contextualism](contextualism-synthesis-c8874149-f3cc-4119-8896-6d48b3a50470.md)

## Content

**Suppose a person believes a proposition based on faulty evidence, and the proposition is accidentally true. Intuitively, this is not a case of knowledge.**, **In everyday contexts, individuals often rely on sources of information that are prone to error, such as memory or testimony.**, **If contextual standards are too lenient in everyday contexts, it might lead to attributing knowledge to someone who merely has lucky true beliefs.**
