# Is a causal connection necessary for knowledge regardless of context?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** a1087417-eb53-4b6c-9f28-f4bd556b5de0
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Contextualism in Epistemology](contextualism-in-epistemology-synthesis-f4c5fe35-58d0-4960-a84a-81d2bc69d122.md)

## Content

**Consider a scenario in which a person claims to know a historical fact like "Julius Caesar was murdered" without any causal connection to the event. Intuitively, we might doubt that they truly know this event without any direct or indirect causal link.**, **There is a plausible principle that knowledge requires some form of causal connection to the fact known. This principle, together with the judgment that the absence of a causal connection undermines knowledge, implies that in some contexts, knowledge claims may be improperly granted when the causal link is absent.**
