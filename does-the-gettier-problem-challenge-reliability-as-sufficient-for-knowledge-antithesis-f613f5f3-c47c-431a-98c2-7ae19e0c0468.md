# Does the Gettier problem challenge reliability as sufficient for knowledge?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** f613f5f3-c47c-431a-98c2-7ae19e0c0468
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Reliability Theory of Knowledge](reliability-theory-of-knowledge-synthesis-fa98d716-d2d9-4374-84c7-532726e6d89c.md)

## Content

**Consider a scenario in which a person, Smith, is justified in believing that Jones owns a Ford because Smith has strong evidence for this belief. However, unknown to Smith, Jones has sold his Ford, and Smith is not aware that he has another belief that someone in his office, Brown, is in Barcelona, which is true. In such a scenario, intuitively we would judge that Smith does not truly know that "someone in the office owns a Ford".**, **A plausible principle is that a justified true belief formed through reliable processes should constitute knowledge. This principle together with Gettier scenarios shows that if someone can have justified true beliefs through a reliable process yet still lack knowledge, reliability might not be sufficient.**
