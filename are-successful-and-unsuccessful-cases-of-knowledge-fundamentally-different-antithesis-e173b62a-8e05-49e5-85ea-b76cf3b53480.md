# Are successful and unsuccessful cases of knowledge fundamentally different?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** e173b62a-8e05-49e5-85ea-b76cf3b53480
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Disjunctivist Theory of Knowledge](disjunctivist-theory-of-knowledge-synthesis-eb3c40bf-59e8-47a3-98db-de4d9568dbcc.md)

## Content

**Consider a case where two people each watch a dog run towards a tree; one sees it successfully climb, while the other misses it due to a fleeting obstruction. Despite different visual experiences, it seems both form the belief that the dog climbed the tree. The difference in visual success seems irrelevant to whether they know the dog climbed the tree, suggesting that successful and unsuccessful cases may not be fundamentally different.**, **A plausible principle is that if two people form a belief based on the same evidence, their knowledge status should not differ dramatically based solely on perceptual success or failure.**
