# Can unreliable but accurate processes undermine knowledge?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 2d7e2f18-c14d-4441-b418-130d82bc685a
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Modified Foundationalism with Non-Conscious Justification](modified-foundationalism-with-non-conscious-justification-synthesis-e080555b-8494-4f36-b1f9-a93cbae9e68f.md)

## Content

**Suppose we have a case in which a person arrives at true beliefs using a method that is generally unreliable but happened to be accurate this time, like a notoriously miscalibrated compass used in favorable conditions. Intuitively, we either have to say that the person knows the direction or that they do not. If we say they know, then we risk endorsing unreliable methods as providing knowledge. If we say they do not know, then true belief dependent on unreliable processes is insufficient for knowledge.**
