# Does a reliable process necessarily guarantee the absence of epistemic luck?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 432d630a-c0d4-4bbb-bdf6-3a183c7d67b1
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Reliability Theory of Knowledge](reliability-theory-of-knowledge-synthesis-74a39329-93a9-479f-b8d3-ff182da0738f.md)

## Content

**Consider an archer who almost always hits the bullseye due to exceptional skill, yet the wind plays a decisive role in the arrow hitting the target in one instance. Intuitively, luck in this instance might undermine the archery scenario as an analogy for knowledge.**, **The archer’s skill represents a reliable process leading to the true belief that the target will be hit.**, **The accuracy in this case occurs due to the interaction of skill and external, unforeseen factors like the wind, akin to luck in achieving a true belief.**, **This analogy suggests a reliable process might still be subject to luck, undermining its sufficiency for knowledge.**
