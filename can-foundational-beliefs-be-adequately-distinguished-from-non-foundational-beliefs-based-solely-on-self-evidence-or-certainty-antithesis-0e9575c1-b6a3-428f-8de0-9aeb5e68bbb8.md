# Can foundational beliefs be adequately distinguished from non-foundational beliefs based solely on self-evidence or certainty?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 0e9575c1-b6a3-428f-8de0-9aeb5e68bbb8
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Evidentialist Foundationalism](evidentialist-foundationalism-synthesis-26c84477-fc91-4190-8a0e-24d90c56393c.md)

## Content

**Consider a belief regarded as self-evident but which is later proven false due to cognitive biases or oversight. In such a scenario, intuitively, self-evidence does not guarantee truth.**, **There is a plausible principle that foundational beliefs should be reliably distinct from those that require further support. If reliance on self-evidence or certainty is the marker, it fails where psychological factors create false certainty.**, **Beliefs considered foundational for their apparent self-evidence or certainty must contend with instances of error that arise naturally despite seeming certainty, thus challenging their foundational role.**
