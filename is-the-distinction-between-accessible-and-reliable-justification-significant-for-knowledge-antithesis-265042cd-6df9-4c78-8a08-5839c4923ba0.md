# Is the distinction between accessible and reliable justification significant for knowledge?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 265042cd-6df9-4c78-8a08-5839c4923ba0
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Disjunctivist Externalism Summary](disjunctivist-externalism-summary-synthesis-838ba4d9-493f-4109-9c69-34614a236108.md)

## Content

**Suppose we have a case in which a person's belief is formed through a process that is typically reliable but provides no accessible evidence to the person. Intuitively, we either have to say that the person knows the belief or that they do not. If we say they know, it raises concerns about accepting beliefs formed in ways that seem unrecognizable as knowledge to us. If we say they do not know, it contradicts the idea that reliable belief suffices for knowledge.**, **Consider a scenario in which a person holds a true belief about the presence of a cat in a room based solely on a reliable gut feeling, without having any other accessible reasons. Intuitively, we struggle to affirm this as knowledge because the person lacks an accessible basis for their belief.**, **It is plausible that without accessible justification, distinguishing between knowledge and mere true belief becomes problematic, thereby challenging the role of reliability alone in the definition of knowledge.**
