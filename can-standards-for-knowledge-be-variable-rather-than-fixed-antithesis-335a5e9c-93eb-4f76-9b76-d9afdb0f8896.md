# Can standards for knowledge be variable rather than fixed?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 335a5e9c-93eb-4f76-9b76-d9afdb0f8896
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Invariantism Summary](invariantism-summary-synthesis-0c8c051d-f89d-468b-87e6-c5a1fb54f910.md)

## Content

**Consider a case where varying contexts involve different degrees of skepticism. For example, in everyday conversation, ordinary claims might be considered known without much scrutiny, whereas in a scientific context, the same claims might require rigorous evidence and testing.**, **A plausible principle is that the standards for knowing can adapt to the requirements of the context. In cases where varying degrees of evidence are required and provided, intuitively, we might judge that what counts as knowledge can change.**, **If standards for knowledge were fixed and invariant, there would be no reason to demand differing degrees of evidence across different contexts.**
