# Can intellectual virtues explain cases where someone seems to know despite lacking explicit justification?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** b7c0440b-cece-4fb7-970a-74e1049e170c
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Virtue Epistemology](virtue-epistemology-synthesis-9deda8b7-9bee-4f9f-90d0-2d2b46259731.md)

## Content

**Suppose we have a case in which a person has a true belief based on an unconscious instinct or gut feeling, which turns out to be reliable, such as a doctor diagnosing a rare disease correctly on intuition alone without conscious reasoning. Intuitively, we either have to say that this person knows due to their reliable instincts or that they do not know because they lack explicit justification. If we say they know due to reliability, then the requirement for intellectual virtues being consciously applied is undermined. If we say they do not know due to lack of explicit justification, then we imply justification must be accessible, contradicting the theoretical view.**
