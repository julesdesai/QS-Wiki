# Can implicit biases influence inferential evidence to such a degree that it impairs knowledge acquisition?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** e7b2f5c7-f5aa-4bae-a23f-b8b90f2c4bef
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Inferential Evidence Theory](inferential-evidence-theory-synthesis-888e1fca-9103-4ede-a19f-61db12f550f6.md)

## Content

**Consider a hiring manager who believes a candidate is the best choice due to a strong resume, even though this belief is unduly influenced by subconscious biases towards the candidate's background. Intuitively, the belief in the candidate's superiority is not knowledge because it is contaminated by bias, not objective inferential evidence.**, **Inferential evidence should ideally be free of personal biases and rely solely on objective reasoning from known premises. If biases taint the reasoning process, they compromise the veracity and reliability required for the belief to count as knowledge.**
