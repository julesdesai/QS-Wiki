# Do certain lottery paradoxes show the inadequacy of probabilistic justification for knowledge?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** 5fb953bb-0755-4d94-877a-84d95dee1f29
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Probabilistic Foundationalism](probabilistic-foundationalism-synthesis-16fe8fa2-69bc-4186-a85e-e3861ba3ba16.md)

## Content

**Suppose a lottery participant holds a belief that a ticket will lose the lottery based on the low probability of winning. Intuitively, this belief, even when true, doesn’t amount to knowledge as it is still probabilistically contingent.**, **If probabilistic justification were sufficient for knowledge, every true belief about a lottery ticket losing would count as knowledge, which contradicts our common intuition about knowledge.**, **The probability, although high, does not convert belief into knowledge without certainty, which seems necessary for knowledge beyond mere belief.**
