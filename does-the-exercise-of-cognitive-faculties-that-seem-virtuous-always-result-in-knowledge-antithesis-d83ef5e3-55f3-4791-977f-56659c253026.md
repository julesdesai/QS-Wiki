# Does the exercise of cognitive faculties that seem virtuous always result in knowledge?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** d83ef5e3-55f3-4791-977f-56659c253026
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Virtue Epistemology Summary](virtue-epistemology-summary-synthesis-eb390c86-5ce7-412f-bf22-00e631bfa2b2.md)

## Content

**The exercise of cognitive faculties that are virtuous should ideally lead to knowledge, but it is implausible to assert that this is always the case.**, **Consider a scenario where a scientist carefully follows ethical and rigorous research methods but mistakenly analyzes data, leading to a false conclusion. Intuitively, despite the virtue in method, knowledge is not achieved.**, **It is plausible to encounter a situation where a person sincerely and thoughtfully considers evidence yet arrives at a false belief. This counter-example challenges the sufficiency of virtuous cognitive exercises for guaranteeing knowledge.**
