# Does intellectual virtue sufficiently address skeptical scenarios?

**Node Type:** antithesis
**Node ID:** af8e3e87-f781-4007-a244-6b08abe52316
**Depth:** 4
**Terminal:** No

**Parent:** [Virtue Epistemology](virtue-epistemology-synthesis-c68e0504-3343-450c-a9ae-cebbb34ec2ad.md)

## Content

**Suppose we have a case in which a person, engaging all intellectual virtues, believes they are not in a simulation. Intuitively, we either have to say that they know this because of their intellect or they do not know it, as their faculties could be misled by the simulation. If we say they know, then such situations trivialize the challenge of skepticism, for even in misleading scenarios, knowledge is granted through intellectual faculties. If we say they do not know, then intellectual virtue seems insufficient in defining knowledge, undermining its role**, **It is implausible that our understanding of knowledge allows people to claim knowledge in skeptical scenarios merely due to proper intellectual virtue, as this would ignore deep epistemic challenges**
